## READING PASSAGE: The Sunlight Soap Story

The first bars of Sunlight soap rolled off a manufacturing line operated by Lever Brothers in England in January 1886. The company was founded by William and James Lever, the only sons among ten siblings, ranking seventh and eighth in birth order. Their father worked as a shopkeeper, and both boys were trained within the family enterprise from a young age. During this period, William quickly distinguished himself through strong commercial instincts and a flair for enterprise. By his mid-twenties, he had effectively taken control of the business and was eager to pursue more ambitious ventures, which ultimately led him to the soap industry.

At that time, soap was a basic necessity, but it was typically sold in plain, unappealing blocks from general stores. William Lever recognised that there was an opportunity to transform this mundane product into something desirable. He believed that if soap could be manufactured to a consistently high quality, given an attractive name and packaging, and marketed aggressively, it would appeal to a growing middle class eager for products that promised cleanliness and respectability. The name "Sunlight" was chosen to evoke purity, freshness, and the natural bleaching power of the sun.

Lever's innovation was not merely in branding but also in production. He invested in the latest manufacturing technology, allowing his factory at Port Sunlight, near Liverpool, to produce soap on a scale never seen before. Port Sunlight was more than a factory; it was a purpose-built village for the workers, with good quality housing, schools, libraries, and recreational facilities. Lever believed that contented workers would be productive workers, and his model village became famous around the world as an example of enlightened industrial paternalism.

The marketing of Sunlight soap was equally revolutionary. Lever was one of the first manufacturers to advertise his product nationally. He used eye-catching posters, newspaper advertisements, and even gave away free samples to entice customers. The advertisements emphasised not just the cleaning power of Sunlight but also the social benefits of using it – cleaner clothes meant a cleaner home, and a cleaner home meant a more respectable family. This appeal to aspiration proved enormously successful.

By the 1890s, Sunlight soap was selling in huge quantities across Britain and in export markets around the world. Lever Brothers expanded rapidly, acquiring other soap companies and diversifying into new products. In 1929, the company merged with Dutch margarine producer Margarine Unie to form Unilever, which today is one of the world's largest consumer goods companies, with products sold in over 190 countries.

William Lever, who was made a baronet in 1911 and later became Viscount Leverhulme, never forgot his principles. He remained committed to the welfare of his workers and to the idea that business should serve society, not just shareholders. He once wrote: "I do not believe that commerce can be permanently successful unless it is conducted on ethical principles." His legacy lives on not only in the global giant that Unilever has become but also in the continued existence of Port Sunlight village, which remains a thriving community more than a century after it was built.

The story of Sunlight soap is more than a tale of commercial success; it is a story of how vision, innovation, and social responsibility can combine to create something lasting. From a small family grocery business in Victorian England, William Lever built an industrial empire by understanding that even the simplest product could be transformed through quality, branding, and a genuine concern for people.
